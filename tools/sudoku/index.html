How to use

  1. Pick a difficulty, theme and tile size, then press New Game.
  2. Tap a cell, then tap a number (or use your keyboard 1–9). Same numbers and the active row, column and box are highlighted to help you scan.
  3. Turn on Notes to pencil in candidates. Check flags conflicting numbers in red. Hint fills one correct cell when you're stuck.
  4. Use Pause to hide the board and stop the clock, and Full for a distraction-free fullscreen board. Your game and best times are saved on this device.
  5. Keyboard: type a digit to fill (1–9, and A–G on 16×16), arrows to move, Delete to erase. Shortcuts: F fullscreen, C check, H hint, N notes, U undo, E erase, P/Space pause. (On 16×16, A–G are digits, so those letter shortcuts give way to number entry.)

100% offline — every puzzle is generated locally and has a single unique solution.

How to solve Sudoku — a complete guide

The examples below use the classic 9×9 grid, but every technique works on the 4×4, 6×6 and 16×16 variants too — just replace "digits 1–9" with the digit range for that grid (1–4, 1–6, or 1–9 plus A–G).

The one rule

Fill the grid so that every row, every column and every box contains each digit exactly once. That's it — a digit may never repeat within the same row, column or box. There is always exactly one solution, so you never have to guess: every cell can be reasoned out.

1. Scanning / cross-hatching Start here

Pick a digit, say 5, and look at where the 5s already are. For each box that's still missing a 5, mentally cross out every row and column that already contains a 5. Often only one cell in the box survives — that cell must be a 5. Repeat for each digit and each box. This alone solves most Easy puzzles.

2. Naked single (the "only choice") Easy

Look at a single empty cell and list which digits are still allowed in it (not already in its row, column or box). If only one digit is possible, that's the answer. Cells with the fewest open neighbours are the best place to hunt.

3. Hidden single Easy

A cell might have several candidates, yet a digit may have only one home in a given row, column or box. If 7 can legally go in just one cell of a box — even if that cell could also hold other numbers — then 7 belongs there. Hidden singles are the most common "aha" after scanning.

💡 Tip: turn on Notes and pencil in candidates once scanning slows down. Seeing the possibilities written out makes the next four techniques obvious.

4. Naked pairs & triples Medium

If two cells in the same unit (row, column or box) have the exact same two candidates — say {3,8} and {3,8} — then 3 and 8 are locked into those two cells. You can safely remove 3 and 8 from every other cell in that unit. The same idea extends to three cells sharing three candidates (a naked triple).

5. Hidden pairs Medium

The mirror image of a naked pair: if two digits can only appear in the same two cells of a unit, those cells must hold those two digits. You can then erase all other candidates from those two cells, which often unlocks them.

6. Pointing pairs (box → line) Hard

Within a box, if a digit's only candidates all sit in one row (or one column), then that digit must come from that box's portion of the line. So you can remove it from the rest of that row/column outside the box — even though you haven't placed it yet.

7. Box/line reduction (line → box) Hard

The reverse: if a digit in a row or column is confined to the cells that also belong to a single box, remove that digit from the rest of that box. Techniques 6 and 7 are the workhorses of Hard puzzles.

8. X-Wing Expert

An advanced pattern: when a digit appears as a candidate in exactly two cells on each of two different rows, and those cells line up in the same two columns, the digit can be removed from those two columns everywhere else. (The same works with rows and columns swapped.) Swordfish and XY-Wing extend the idea further for the very hardest grids.

A reliable solving routine

  • Scan each digit 1–9 across all boxes for easy placements.
  • Fill every naked and hidden single you can find.
  • When stuck, pencil in all candidates and look for naked/hidden pairs.
  • Use pointing pairs and box/line reduction to trim candidates further.
  • Only reach for X-Wing-style patterns on Expert puzzles.
  • Never guess — if you're stuck, a logical step exists; the Hint button can show you one cell to get unstuck.
Smaller grids (4×4, 6×6) are perfect for learning these patterns; 16×16 is the same logic at a larger scale, so master the 9×9 first.
